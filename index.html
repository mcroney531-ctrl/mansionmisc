Before You Enter

You Are the Facilitator

A professional development session is underway. Two learners are presenting research. Something in this room may not be what it seems.

The Situation

AI hallucinations are outputs that sound completely real — specific, confident, well-formatted — but have no factual basis. They don't announce themselves. They don't flicker or glitch. They walk right through the wall like they belong there, wearing the clothes of credible information.

AI-assisted work has become a normal part of how people research, write, prepare, and present. For many educators and L&D professionals, this is actually how AI first shows up in their world — not as a new platform to learn or a policy to navigate, but as content. Someone else's content, already prepared, already in front of an audience. AI fluency begins with recognizing that moment for what it is. What matters then is what happens next — whether the output gets a second look before it gets passed along. Hallucinations thrive in that gap between what the AI said and what anyone stopped to verify.

Your Scenario

Your organization is running a PD session on personality frameworks and participant dynamics in workshop settings — specifically, how to manage difficult moments when learners disengage or push back. Two learners, Lynn and Russ, have each prepared a short research presentation.

Both used AI in their process. That's where the story diverges. One leaned on it as a source. The other used it as a starting point and went further. As the facilitator, you won't be told which is which — your job is to read the room, evaluate what's on the slide, and respond.

You're not grading their presentations. You're practicing the habit of pausing before you accept what confident-sounding information puts in front of you — because in a live session, what gets through shapes what your learners walk away believing.

One Room Cleared

Notice what just happened. The slide looked professional. The claim was specific. The room was nodding. And the source — when it finally surfaced — was a chatbot's confident answer, validated by nothing but the presenter's own experience.

That's the anatomy of a hallucination in the wild: it doesn't look wrong. It looks exactly like everything else.

One more learner is waiting to present. Fair warning — not everything in this mansion is a ghost.

The Light Stays On

Two learners. Both used AI. Both mentioned it casually. Only one treated it as the source.

Learner A let a confident answer become a fact — her own experience felt like confirmation, and the AI felt like proof. Two feelings, zero verification. Learner B used AI the way you'd use a knowledgeable colleague: it pointed him somewhere, and he went there himself.

The skill you practiced here was never "spot the lie." Sometimes the slide is clean. The skill is the pause — the willingness to ask "how do you know?" of any claim, any tool, and yourself. Especially when it already tracks with your experience.

Take It With You
The next time something confident lands in front of you — from an AI, a colleague, or your own intuition — give it the half-second pause this mansion gave you. Verification isn't distrust. It's the light you carry into every room.
